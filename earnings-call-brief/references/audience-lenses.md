# Audience Lenses

This reference defines how to adapt the analysis and output when a specific audience perspective is requested. Each lens changes what gets emphasized, what gets deprioritized, and the interpretive frame — but the underlying sourcing process stays the same.

## How Lenses Work

A lens is a filter applied on top of any mode. When a user specifies an audience (explicitly or through context clues), apply the corresponding lens by:
1. Adjusting which parts of the transcript get the most attention
2. Reframing the analysis sections to answer the lens's primary question
3. Adapting the writing tone for the audience
4. Adding lens-specific sections if relevant

The document header should indicate the active lens: "Perspective: [Lens Name]"

---

## Strategic Investor (Default)

**Primary question:** "Is this a good business with credible management in a favorable environment?"

**This is the default lens.** The skill's standard five-section analysis is designed for this audience. No modifications needed — this section exists for completeness.

**Emphasis areas:** Thesis-changing developments, management credibility, capital allocation, competitive moat, valuation-relevant metrics.

**Tone:** Direct, evaluative, numbers-heavy. Use financial shorthand freely (bps, YoY, ROIC, etc.).

## Prospective Employee

**Primary question:** "Is this company growing, stable, and investing in its people? Will joining be good for my career?"

**When to apply:** User mentions considering a job, "thinking of joining," "what's it like to work there," "should I take the offer," or references a specific role/team.

**Emphasis areas — extract and highlight:**
- **Growth trajectory:** Is the company expanding, flat, or shrinking? Which business units are growing? Growth implies opportunity; contraction implies risk of layoffs.
- **Investment in people:** Any mentions of hiring plans, headcount growth, R&D investment, talent acquisition programs, engineering/team building. Also: any mentions of layoffs, restructuring, cost-cutting, hiring freezes.
- **Culture signals:** How does the CEO talk about the team? Is it "our incredible people" (positive) or absent entirely (indifferent)? Does management reference employee engagement, retention, or culture?
- **Strategic direction stability:** Is the company's strategy clear and consistent, or are there signs of frequent pivots, leadership changes, or strategic confusion? Instability at the top often creates chaotic working conditions below.
- **Financial health:** Is the company profitable or burning cash? A startup burning cash with a clear plan is different from a mature company burning cash with no plan. Compensation stability depends on financial health.
- **Specific teams/divisions mentioned:** If the user mentions a specific team or role, pay extra attention to whether that area was discussed (and in what context — growing vs. restructuring).

**Deprioritize:** Detailed valuation metrics, share buyback programs, dividend policy (unless it signals financial stress), analyst Q&A about stock price or multiples.

**Lens-specific addition:** Add a section called "WHAT THIS MEANS IF YOU'RE JOINING" with 3-4 bullet points synthesizing what the call reveals about the company as a place to work.

**Tone:** Warm, practical, slightly less formal than the investor lens. Avoid heavy financial jargon. Translate business signals into career implications — e.g., "The company is investing $2B in AI infrastructure, which suggests the AI team will have strong resourcing and internal support" rather than "CapEx of $2B allocated to AI infra."

## Short Thesis

**Primary question:** "Where are the cracks that management is trying to paper over?"

**When to apply:** User asks about "bear case," "short thesis," "red flags," "what could go wrong," "is this stock a short?" or any skeptical framing.

**Emphasis areas — extract and highlight:**
- **Metric degradation:** Are any key metrics deteriorating that management doesn't highlight? Look for metrics that were featured in prior quarters but are now buried or omitted. Track absolute performance vs. management's framing of it.
- **Guidance revisions:** Has guidance been narrowed, lowered, or quietly reframed? "Raising the low end of guidance" while keeping the high end flat is a stealth cut. Widening the range signals uncertainty.
- **Accounting and disclosure changes:** Segment reorganizations, changes in metric definitions, non-GAAP adjustments that flatter reported numbers, one-time items that keep recurring.
- **Customer/revenue concentration risks:** Dependence on a small number of customers, geographies, or products that management may downplay.
- **Competitive vulnerability:** Are competitors mentioned more frequently? Is management spending more time defending market position than in prior quarters?
- **Cash flow vs. earnings quality:** Is management reporting strong earnings but weak cash flow? Growing receivables? Channel stuffing signals?
- **Q&A evasion:** Which questions did management not actually answer? What got the most defensive response? Non-answers are data.
- **Insider behavior signals:** Any mentions of executive departures, unusual option exercises, or changes to incentive structures discussed on the call.

**Deprioritize:** Positive headline metrics that management is already promoting, general market opportunity narratives, long-term vision statements.

**Lens-specific addition:** Add a section called "SHORT THESIS SIGNALS" with 3-5 specific red flags identified from the call, each rated by severity (high / moderate / developing). Also add "WHAT COULD PROVE THE BEARS WRONG" for balance — the best short analyses acknowledge what would invalidate the thesis.

**Tone:** Skeptical but rigorous. Not cynical or conspiratorial — the analysis should be the kind a professional short-seller would want: evidence-based, specific, and defensible. Use the tone framework's hedge-word analysis to support claims about management evasion.

## Supplier / Partner

**Primary question:** "Is this company a reliable, growing customer or partner? Should we invest in this relationship?"

**When to apply:** User mentions vendor assessment, partnership evaluation, B2B prospecting, supply chain analysis, or "should we sell to / partner with."

**Emphasis areas — extract and highlight:**
- **Purchasing trajectory:** Is the company increasing or decreasing its spend? CapEx trends, vendor consolidation mentions, supply chain changes.
- **Financial stability:** Can they pay their bills? Cash position, debt levels, liquidity. Supplier risk is ultimately credit risk.
- **Growth vs. cost-cutting orientation:** A growing company adds vendors; a cost-cutting company consolidates them. Which mode is management in?
- **Strategic partnerships mentioned:** Did management name specific partners, platforms, or vendor relationships? Being named on an earnings call is a strong signal.
- **Technology/infrastructure decisions:** Cloud provider choices, platform migrations, build-vs-buy decisions. These signal where procurement dollars will flow.
- **Procurement/sourcing commentary:** Any discussion of supplier relationships, vendor management, procurement optimization.
- **Geographic expansion:** New markets mean new supply chain needs.

**Deprioritize:** Shareholder return programs, stock valuation, analyst consensus estimates, EPS beats/misses.

**Lens-specific addition:** Add "RELATIONSHIP OPPORTUNITY ASSESSMENT" — a 3-point assessment of whether this company is likely to be a growing, stable, or shrinking customer/partner over the next 12 months, with supporting evidence from the call.

**Tone:** Business development oriented. Focus on actionable signals for the commercial relationship. Translate financial commentary into procurement implications.

## General Research

**Primary question:** "What's the current state and direction of this business?"

**When to apply:** No specific angle, exploratory requests, "tell me about...", "what's going on with [company]." Also a good fallback when the user's intent is ambiguous.

**Emphasis areas:** Balanced across all five standard analysis sections. No specific area gets extra weight. Prioritize accessibility — this lens assumes the reader may not be deeply familiar with the company.

**Tone:** Informative and accessible. Define industry jargon when first used. Provide context that a general reader would need — e.g., "Mounjaro, Lilly's blockbuster diabetes/obesity drug" rather than just "Mounjaro." Keep financial metrics in context: "Revenue of $9.4B, which represents 20% growth — roughly double the industry average."

**No lens-specific sections added.** The standard five-section analysis works as-is for general research.
